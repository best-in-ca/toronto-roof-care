Severe Weather Roof Preparation

Before, during, and after severe weather. What to do, what to avoid, and how to give your roof the best chance.

Toronto gets four kinds of severe weather that affect roofs: thunderstorms with hail, high-wind events, ice storms, and heavy snow. Each one calls for different preparation. The homeowner who's done basic prep before the event and knows how to respond after recovers faster, files better insurance claims, and avoids cascade damage.

Before any severe weather (year-round)

Baseline documentation

  • Date-stamped photos of all four sides of your roof, taken in good light. Update annually
  • Photos of the eavestroughs, soffits, and fascia
  • Note the install date of the current roof and its warranty terms
  • Save the emergency contact number for your roofer in your phone: (416) 826-0040 for The Roof Technician

Annual roof check

  • Schedule professional inspection if last one was 12+ months ago
  • Address any known issues — loose shingles, damaged flashing — before severe weather season
  • Verify attic ventilation is working properly
  • Clean eavestroughs and downspouts

Insurance documentation

  • Know what your home insurance covers for roof damage
  • Save policy contact information and claim line phone number
  • Understand the deductible for roof claims
  • Many policies have age limits on roof replacement coverage — confirm yours

Thunderstorms with hail

Before

  • Move outdoor furniture, planters, and BBQs away from the house
  • Park vehicles in garage if possible
  • Bring in anything that could become a projectile

During

  • Stay inside, away from windows
  • If hail is golf ball-sized or larger, expect possible roof damage
  • Note the time and duration of the event for insurance purposes

After

  • Visually inspect the home from inside (looking up at ceilings) within 30 minutes
  • Wait until the weather clears, then visually inspect from outside (from the ground)
  • Look for: dents in the eavestrough (sign of impact severity), damaged AC unit fins, broken windows, vegetation damage
  • Photograph anything visible
  • If significant damage suspected, contact insurance within 24 hours
  • Don't get on the roof yourself — professional inspection is safer and produces better documentation

High-wind events

Before

  • Trim any tree branches within 10 feet of the home
  • Secure loose outdoor items (toys, garden tools, light furniture)
  • Close garage doors firmly
  • If wind gusts over 80 km/h are forecast, expect potential roof damage

During

  • Listen for any unusual sounds — flapping, scraping, banging on the roof
  • Stay away from windows on the windward side
  • Take note if any shingles or debris is visible flying from the roof

After

  • Walk the perimeter looking for: missing shingles, lifted shingles, damaged flashing, fallen tree limbs
  • Check eavestroughs for damage and detachment
  • Look at top-floor ceilings for any new water staining
  • Document damage with photos before any cleanup
  • If significant damage, contact emergency repair for stabilization

Ice storms

Before

  • Trim weak tree branches that could fail under ice load
  • Move vehicles away from trees if possible
  • Confirm emergency repair contact numbers are accessible
  • Stock up on basics for possible power outage

During

  • Stay inside — do not attempt to remove ice from anywhere
  • Listen for cracking sounds from trees nearby
  • If the home loses power, check that pipes don't freeze

After

  • Stay clear of the house until ice begins melting (falling ice is dangerous)
  • Look for: fallen branches on roof, damaged eavestroughs from ice weight, broken roofing materials
  • Don't try to remove icicles or ice dams yourself
  • Document damage as conditions allow
  • Schedule professional inspection if damage is suspected

Heavy snow

Before

  • Modern Toronto roofs handle normal snow loads without intervention
  • Only consider proactive snow removal if you have: flat roof with poor drainage, unusual accumulation, or specific structural concerns

During and after

  • Monitor ceiling for any sagging or new staining
  • Watch for icicles forming at eaves (sign of ice dam formation)
  • If using a roof rake to clear lower portions, work from the ground only — never climb on a snow-loaded roof
  • If snow load is genuinely concerning, contact a professional. Don't go on the roof yourself

The emergency response sequence

If there's active water entry during or after a storm:

  • Step 1: Contain interior damage. Place buckets, lift carpets, move furniture and electronics
  • Step 2: Document with date-stamped photos before any cleanup
  • Step 3: If a ceiling drywall area is sagging from water, drill a small hole to drain — prevents larger collapse
  • Step 4: Call emergency repair: The Roof Technician at (416) 826-0040
  • Step 5: Notify insurance within 24 hours of damage discovery
  • Step 6: Don't attempt rooftop work yourself — wet, damaged roofs aren't safe

Insurance claim coordination

For successful insurance claims after severe weather:

  • File the claim within the time window your policy specifies (often 24–48 hours for prompt notification)
  • Provide documented photos from before and after
  • Don't authorize permanent repairs until the adjuster has inspected
  • Get an independent contractor estimate as a check against the adjuster's number
  • Don't sign anything that transfers your claim rights to a contractor
  • See the full insurance claims guide for the process

What insurance covers (typically)

  • Hail damage to shingles (if recent and documented)
  • Wind damage to shingles (usually wind threshold applies)
  • Fallen tree or branch damage (if tree wasn't dead/dying)
  • Active water damage from sudden events

What insurance doesn't cover (typically)

  • Age-related deterioration
  • Maintenance failure (no eavestrough cleaning, no professional inspections)
  • Slow leaks that worsened over time
  • Damage from a previously-known issue you didn't address
