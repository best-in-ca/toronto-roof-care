Toronto Roof Seasonal Care Guide

Month-by-month maintenance for Toronto roofs. The five-minute habits that compound into a 25-year lifespan.

A Toronto roof faces something different every season — UV cooking in July, hail in May, ice dams in February, wind in November. Each season has specific care that matters more than others. This guide walks through what to do month-by-month so you stay ahead of issues instead of reacting to them.

Winter (December–February)

The harshest season. Most maintenance is observational, not hands-on.

December — Document baseline

  • Take photos of the roof from each side before the first heavy snow
  • Note the date of last professional inspection
  • Save the emergency contact number visibly in case of leaks: The Roof Technician

January — Monitor ice dams

  • After every thaw-freeze cycle, look at the eaves from the ground
  • Ice ridges forming on the eaves are warning signs of inadequate attic ventilation or insulation
  • Don't attempt to remove ice dams yourself — never safe
  • If active leaks appear, call emergency repair immediately. Don't wait for spring

February — Document any winter damage

  • If any leaks or stains appeared, photograph and date them
  • Note where on the roof and what conditions triggered them
  • This becomes reference for spring repair scheduling

Spring (March–May)

The diagnostic season. Winter damage becomes visible. Schedule repairs before storm season.

March — Ground-level inspection

  • Once snow melts, walk the perimeter looking up at the roof
  • Check for: missing shingles, lifted shingles, granule loss, damaged flashing visible from below
  • Look at eavestroughs for sagging or detachment from winter ice
  • Note any ceiling stains that appeared over winter

April — Schedule inspection and repairs

  • Book a professional inspection if last one was 12+ months ago
  • Address any winter damage now — repair contractors get busy in May
  • If you're at year 18+ on the roof's life, start planning replacement

May — Eavestrough cleanout, repair work

  • Clear all gutters of winter debris — twigs, leaves, granules
  • Check downspouts flow freely; clear any blockages
  • Repair work scheduled now should complete before storm season
  • If considering new skylight installation, book early — peak season starts in June

Summer (June–August)

The dry-weather season. Major projects happen, UV damage accumulates, monitor visually.

June — Storm response

  • Severe Toronto thunderstorms hit June through August
  • After hail or wind events, photograph the property for potential insurance claims
  • Document any visible damage immediately
  • Address damaged shingles within 2 weeks — don't let small issues compound

July — UV monitoring

  • Look for shingles showing curl, cup, or fade — UV damage shows by mid-summer
  • Algae streaking on north-facing slopes shows growth now
  • Walk through attic on a hot day. Heat over 60°C indicates inadequate ventilation

August — Major project window

  • Best weather window for replacements and major repairs
  • If you've been deferring major work, schedule it now for September completion
  • Book contractors by mid-August for September starts

Fall (September–November)

The most critical maintenance season. Everything you skip will affect winter.

September — Pre-winter inspection

  • Book a professional inspection if you haven't yet this year
  • Time the work so any needed repairs happen before October
  • Document attic ventilation condition — adjustments are easier now
  • September also has the best curing conditions for paint or sealant work

October — Final pre-winter prep

  • Final eavestrough cleanout after most leaves have fallen
  • Attic check from underneath — look for any moisture, mould, blocked vents
  • Verify bath and dryer exhausts vent properly to the exterior
  • Secure any loose roof items (satellite dish, antenna mounts)

November — Last warm-weather window

  • Any remaining repair work must complete before mid-month
  • By November 15, exterior work becomes weather-dependent
  • Confirm emergency contact numbers in your phone before winter
  • If considering a new roof and you're at year 20+, book the spring slot now

Weather-event response

Toronto sees specific weather events that require specific responses:

Heavy rain / thunderstorms

Check attic for any new moisture within 24 hours of the event. Look at ceilings on top floor. Document anything immediately.

Hail

Document with photos within 24 hours. Look at eavestrough for dents (sign of impact severity). Visible shingle damage may justify insurance claim. See emergency prep.

High winds

After any wind event over 60 km/h, look for missing or lifted shingles. Address within 2 weeks. Single missing shingles let water reach the underlayment — fixable but don't ignore.

Ice storms

Don't go on or near the roof during ice events. After thaw, look for any branches that have fallen onto the roof, damaged flashing, broken eavestroughs.

Snow accumulation

Most Toronto roofs handle normal snow loads without intervention. Only consider snow removal if you have an unusually flat roof, heavy accumulation (over 2 ft), or specific structural concerns. Use a roof rake from the ground, never climb on a snow-loaded roof.

What changes by roof age

New roof (years 0–5)

Minimal maintenance needed beyond gutter cleaning. Warranty covers most issues.

Mid-life (years 5–15)

Standard seasonal care. Occasional repair work. Annual gutter cleaning essential.

Late-life (years 15–20)

Increased vigilance. Professional inspections become more important. Budget for repairs.

End-of-life (years 20+)

Plan replacement. See roof replacement services and 2026 shingle comparison.
